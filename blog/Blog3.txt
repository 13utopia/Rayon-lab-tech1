## Blog Content

Laboratory processes can generate fumes, vapours, odours, dust, and airborne contaminants. Selecting the correct fume-control system is essential for creating a safer working environment.

A laboratory fume hood, fume spot extractor, exhaust blower, and scrubber perform different functions. They should not be selected only according to price or available space.

The correct system depends on the substances being handled, process quantity, exposure risk, required airflow, exhaust route, filtration, laboratory layout, and applicable safety requirements.

### What Is a Laboratory Fume Hood?

A laboratory fume hood is an enclosed workspace designed to capture contaminants generated during chemical handling and direct them into an exhaust or treatment system.

A fume hood may include:

* Enclosed working chamber
* Movable front sash
* Worktop
* Internal lining
* Utility points
* Lighting
* Airflow monitoring
* Exhaust connection
* Storage beneath the unit

Laboratory fume hoods are generally used where chemical reactions, solvent handling, acid work, heating, or other processes may release hazardous vapours.

### What Is a Fume Spot Extractor?

A fume spot extractor captures contaminants close to a specific point of generation. It commonly includes an adjustable extraction arm that can be positioned near the process.

Spot extractors may be suitable for localised, low-volume activities where full enclosure is not required. Their suitability must still be evaluated according to the substance and process risk.

### What Is an Exhaust Blower?

An exhaust blower creates the airflow required to move contaminated air through the ducting system.

The blower must be selected according to:

* Required air volume
* Duct length
* Number of bends
* Pressure loss
* Hood size
* Chemical compatibility
* Exhaust-discharge requirements

An incorrectly selected blower may create insufficient or unstable airflow.

### What Is a Scrubber?

A scrubber is used to treat selected contaminants in the exhaust air before discharge. The appropriate scrubber type depends on the chemical composition, concentration, airflow, and treatment requirements.

A scrubber should be designed as part of the complete exhaust system rather than added without technical evaluation.

### Important Selection Factors

Before selecting a fume-control system, identify:

1. Chemicals and processes involved
2. Quantity and frequency of use
3. Required containment level
4. Available exhaust route
5. Laboratory airflow conditions
6. Duct and blower requirements
7. Treatment or filtration requirements
8. Maintenance and inspection needs

### Conclusion

A laboratory fume hood provides enclosed containment, a fume spot extractor captures contaminants near a local source, an exhaust blower moves air through the system, and a scrubber treats suitable contaminants before discharge.
